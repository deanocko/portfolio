Integrating Sustainability into Engineering: A Reflective Account from a Nutanix Internship
For the main group project during my internship, we were tasked with developing a dynamic dashboard that would primarily be used to help managers understand the needs of their team in real-time. The goal of the dashboard was for the manager to be able to ascertain, at a glance, all of the members of their team who may have needed assistance with a complex case and those best able to offer support. Second to this primary goal, the dashboard was to display other helpful metrics and reminders for tasks that needed to be completed at specific times. After hours, the dashboard should display staff achievements, birthdays and other news about Nutanix. We were informed that the dashboard would run continuously on all of the TVs in the office, and to accommodate this, we were set up with a virtual machine (VM) to host our designed solution.

As our intern group started to flesh out the project requirements and build a framework for the dashboard, I became concerned about the energy consumption of having TVs operate 24/7, the constant use of power required by the over-provisioned VM we had been supplied and the likelihood that the LCD displays would burn out if they were not refreshed or powered off periodically. I proposed to one of the managers that we could set the dashboard and TVs to run on a schedule aligned with the work day, with a buffer window on either side for any employees who arrived early or left the office after hours. This would allow Nutanix to save money on its electricity costs, reduce its environmental footprint, and prolong the life of the TVs displaying the dashboard. I could only see upsides with my idea and was taken aback when the idea was brushed off, and we were told that difference would be negligible. I was discouraged that the idea wasn't properly considered and felt less motivated to work on the project as I felt we didn't have any agency over design elements or were able to make recommendations.

One of the other interns who had liked the idea of scheduling the TVs suggested that we could also reduce the power consumption of the VM by only using the computational resources required for our project. They realised we could also create custom startup and shut-down scripts that would allow the VM to deactivate during the same time frame as the TVs, allowing for even greater energy savings. This would also give us an opportunity to prove to our mentors that we could apply the skills we had learnt during the internship to manage VMs using hyper-converged infrastructure (HCI). I was impressed that another intern had reflected and followed up with their own ideas to have a positive impact. Schön (1983) emphasises the importance of reflective practice in professional development, allowing for a critical analysis of our actions and their alignment with broader sustainability goals.

Feeling emboldened by the solidarity of my colleague, we decided to present the idea to another manager who we thought might be more receptive. To our surprise, the manager was very supportive of the idea and agreed that our recommendation was a win-win for Nutanix, the environment and our own skill development. He also commended us on considering all of the consequences of our work, as they hadn't previously considered the pixel burn-in on the LCD displays, which would ultimately make the dashboards useless if they couldn’t operate. Ultimately, our dashboard was scheduled to power itself off for 10 hours a day, leading to energy savings of approximately 40% and potentially adding years of life to the TVs it was displayed on.

Fien, Maclean, and Park (2009) argue for integrating sustainability into the engineering curriculum to ensure that future engineers are properly equipped to address the challenges of sustainable development. The challenge was not just identifying sustainability issues but finding feasible solutions within our project constraints that our superiors were willing to adopt. This critical engagement aligns with Boud, Keogh, and Walker’s (1985) view on reflective writing, encouraging a proactive stance towards diagnosing and solving problems.

This experience during my internship reaffirmed that every project should have sustainability principles in mind, no matter how small or seemingly insignificant. Notably, it highlighted the role of engineers in contributing to a sustainable future. Moving forward, I aim to continue advocating for and implementing sustainable practices in all my endeavours. The lessons learned during my internship at Nutanix have solidified my resolve to prioritise sustainability as a priority and an integral part of my professional identity and practice.

References
